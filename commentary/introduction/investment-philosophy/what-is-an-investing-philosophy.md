# What does it mean to have an investment philosophy? 


Valuation is not a science (hard rules and clear outcomes), nor is it an art (there are rules: a company's value is not in the eye of the beholder).

I like to steal Aswath Damodaran's view that valuation is a "craft". 
It contains elements of science (irrefutable logic, e.g. a company's value is the present value of future cash flows).
It also contains elements of art (because of the inherent uncertainty, we are required to use skills such as intuition and creativity to make sense of the unknown).

Words like "creativity" and "intuition" are often taboo in investing circles, where there is an obsession with precision (point estimates and precise price targets), but nonetheless they are skills that all fundamental investors use, whether they admit it or not.

There are many different approaches to being a successful investor. However, the human brain is limited in its capacity. One person cannot master every approach to investing. 

In my view, a good investing philosophy has three characteristics:
1) Logical assumptions about the behaviour of markets
2) Fits the investor's inidividual psychology
3) Is dynamic and evolving, but adheres to core principles that are timeless

Alongside a philosophy comes an investment approach (i.e., what do you actually invest in?)
